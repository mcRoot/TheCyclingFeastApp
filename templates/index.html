The Cycling Feast

Road cycling and mountain biking have been steadily growing in popularity in recent years due to the beneficial effects on health and well-being. This has been fueling an increasing interest in services for amateur riders, ranging from professional training programs, "cycling feasts" as well as travel-agency services and bike and component manufactures. Getting data to help highlight the trend of "novel recruits" can be fundamental to refine the aforementioned services and provide a better target for business.

Among the others, Strava, dubbed as "The Social Network of Athletes" is by far the most well-regarded Web and mobile platform adopted by cyclists to track their activities. Since its start in 2009, both recreational and professional cyclists have been increasingly used the service to upload their activities and connect (or follow) others. One of the most peculiar features is that of segments: users can create public segments, custom road/off-road paths (corresponding to roads and trails in the real world) where community members can compete to obtain the best time and become the KOM (King of the Mountain) or QOM (Queen of the Mountain).

Though most of the data is (reasonably) kept strictly private, data related to segment attempts is publicly available (in an anonymized form) via Strava REST API, allowing in principle to infer trends on the popularity of cycling as a recreational sport among people. More specifically, we exploited the API to download the details of more than 8.000 hill and mountain climbs located in Italy: we chose Italy because of its "reputation" as a region of cyclists, furthermore we focused just on climbs as those represent both a dream and a fear for any amateur and, among the others, one of the reasons for a fan of cycling and/or mountain biking to ride her bike. Additionally, as can be intuitively seen in the image below

Italy is mostly hills and mountains, precisely hills and mountains account for around 80% of the whole Italy's territory, as such we can safely state that climbs alone, beyond being very popular among cyclists, give quite a representative sample of "geographical riding habits" in Italy. So that is why, in the end, we believe that climbs can be a good starting point to depict a representative trend of the whole amateur cycling community.

Along with section details, such as average grade, length, elevation difference and so far, we downloaded also details of any single attempt on these segments in the time frame 2009-2017: preliminary data analysis showed a pretty clear growing trend of new bike riders over recent years and a strong correlation between turistic locations and concentration of rides. Also, time of the year and day of the week represent good indicators to discover popularity of a particular place among cyclists.

The ultimate goal of the project is to leverage machine learning techniques to devise regression models able to predict the trend (the amount) of bike rides is specific locations, as base brick to develop an application to suggest the best places in Italy for cycling fans to go riding.

The Project

Here we present and discuss every detail related to the project, from the dataset exploited to the analysis performed in order to develop the predictive model exploited in the actual application.

The Dataset

The dataset has been generated via exploitation of the Strava REST API.

We focused on Segments data structure in order to explore and gather the data of public segments located in Italy along with the corresponding efforts: this data is publicly available once you register on Strava since does not contain sensitive information about platform' users. In particular, we used these REST endpoint:

  • segment explorer, in order to scan the geographical area of interest for relevant segments;
  • list efforts so as to collect the comprehensive list of efforts for every segment previously collected via the segment explorer endpoint.


We limited the scope of the search to climb segments as hills and mountains can be a well representative sample of road cyclists and mountain bikers' behavior in Italy. Data as been collected using Python, exploiting requests module and leveraging the flexibility of Pandas so as to arrange the dataset in csv files. The resulting files and their corresponding content is described in the following sections.

Segments

Segments are road paths created by Strava users that can be shared with other members: riders can virtually "compete" on these segments to complete them in the shortest time possible. Segments' data can be found at strava_segments_italy.csv.
The file contains the details of around 6000 "climb" segments located in Italy covering mostly the Appennines the Alps. For each segment, a record is provided, showing several features:

  • a unique integer id of the segment;
  • the name of the segment (e.g. Mont Blanc climb);
  • the average grade;
  • the climb category, from 1 (easiest) to 5 (the so called "horse categorie");
  • the total distance in meters;
  • the elevation difference in meters;
  • the data related to start and end latitude and longitude.

Efforts

The data related to efforts is organized in a series of csv files, containing the details of around 6,000,000 different efforts on the 6000 climbs downloaded. An effort corresponds to a unique attempt at completing a segment by an athlete. The downloaded efforts correspond to rides made mostly in the period from 2009 - December 2017, though, due to data errors, there are more than 200 activities featuring unlikely years, either way too in the past (1970) or in the future (2019 and further!): this is probably caused by wrong datetime setup on bike computers or smartphones used by athletes to upload their activities.

Some or the relevant data for each effort:

  • the anonymized athlete name (only first name displayed);
  • the start date in GMT timezone along with the start date in local timezone;
  • the elapsed time (in seconds), i.e. the total time spent on the segment since start datetime;
  • the moving time (in seconds), i.e. the total time the rider was actually pedalling (this time is usually equal or less than elapsed time);
  • the rank, i.e. the position of the effort on the leaderboard, based on elapsed time, where 1 means KOM/QOM (King of the Mountain/Queen of the Mountain).

Analysis

We initially analyzed how amateur cycling has been evolving in Italy over the last years, selecting the period 2009-2017 as a reference time frame.

Number of Kilometers Ridden

First of all, we focussed on the total number of kilometers ridden by Strava's users over the reference period.

As we can see, the growth has been sort of exponential: in particular, focusing on the most recent years (2015-2017), the number of kilometers traveled doubled from 1.5 millions to almost 4 millions.

Of course, we are aware that this exponential trend can suffer from a "technological bias" caused by an increased availability of gps-enabled devices (bike computers, smartphone) that have made it possible to ease the recording of sport activities on online services such as Strava. In other words, while in 2009 the number of such Internet-ready devices was low, their availability was with no doubt much higher in 2015-2017, leading to a possible "technological bias" in the figures.
However, even though the number and the availability of such devices in 2009 may even have been quite low, starting from 2015, we have witnessed a relative large diffusion and availability of Internet-enabled devices, so that we can assume no "technological bias" affecting the data from 2015 on.

A better measure would perhaps be distance traveled per year normalized by total number of users registered to the platform in a given year: unfortunately Strava does not disclose this information.

Geolocated Number of Training Sessions

Next, we analyzed the number of training sessions (efforts) per segment, geolocated on a map, so as to define a preliminary spatial correlation between users' efforts and segments where the rides took place.

The chart above shows the geolocated count of segment efforts in four different years taken as reference:
  • 2009, selected as the year of the official kickoff of Strava, so that we going to regard efforts' quantities on this year as a sort of baseline for subsequent years.
  • 2013, as we can suppose that the availability of gps-enabled sport devices was large enough so as to represent no technological barrier to athletes to access online services such as Strava.
  • Finally, 2015 and 2017 represent the very recent past allowing to understand what kind of growth in the amateur cyclist community one can expected over a shorter timespan (2 years).
Geolocalization on the map was devised by taking start latitude and longitude of each segment and plotting a dot in the corresponding point in the map. As we come closer to 2017, we can see that the number of dots increases (do not take color into account for now): that accounts for the effect of new segments added by users that very same year, in other words segments not available previously. As a consequence, just by looking at the density of dots, we can realized that, while the earlier years 2009 and 2013 witnessed a remarkable growth in the number of segments added to Strava, in 2015 and 2017 the number of new segments reached a "plateau": indeed, looking at 2013 versus 2015 and 2015 versus 2017, it is hard to spot a growth in dots' density. This effect is with no doubt due also to the fact that once a mountain or hill pass got covered by someone who created a segment and publicly shared it, there is no longer need to create a new segment for it. This also tells us that in the space of few years the whole Italy got completely covered on Strava.

The most interesting data, anyway, lies in the color of dots: lighter ones denote a higher number of efforts made on them in the given year. Looking at the evolution depicted in the map it is easy to recognize a strongly growing trend that, we think, can be regarded as a good candidate predictor of the trend in coming years. Indeed, since technology (gps and Internet-enabled bike devices) does not represent anymore a barrier for people to join communities like Strava and, consequently, cannot be regarded as a bias on the data, we expect the coming years being revelatory for such a trend and, ultimately, a good reason to further investigate and analyse.

Distribution of Rides by Month

Let's analyze now how users' rides are distributed over the different months of the year: the chart below shows the distribution of rides by month on the whole dataset, considering the time frame 2009-2017.

It is sort of easy to recognize that most of the rides in Italy occurs during summertime (June - August) with a peak in July. Also, riders seems to be quite active during late Spring (May) and early Fall.

This is as well quite understandable as Italy is located in temperate zone. It also proves, as we were expecting, that climate has a strong influence on riders' habits: along this path, future correlations with historical weather records would be helpful in devising accurate predictive models.

Let's analyse now riders' habits over the week by looking at the way rides are distributed among days of week. Again, the chart below shows the distribution of rides by day of week considering the whole dataset.

Again, it should come as no surprise that most of the riders active on Strava focus their rides in the weekend, most notably on Sunday: accordingly, day of week can be regarded as a relevant predictor of the number of future rides.

Distribution of Rides by Geographical Area

Additionally, in order to see how a specific geographical area might affect the number of rides, we analyzed the density of rides in the different italian districts over the usual 2009-2017 period.
In order to do so we exploited Carto, an online, "location intelligence" app.

A few patterns can be notices from the above geo-localized plot.

First of all, amateur cyclists seem to be more active in North/Central Italy, with the notable exception of east Sicily.
Moreover, The Alps, and expecially Dolomites (Northmost region of Italy), show a stunning density of rides: this could be for instance explained by the fact that Dolomites are one of the UNESCO's world heritage sites, visited every year by hundreds of thousands of people from all around the World.
The same can apply also to Tuscany and the renowned "Cinque Terre" that both show a very high density of rides.

In the end, we believe that geographical location of Strava's segments (latitude/longitude) plays also a strong role in predicting future number of rides.

Influence of Accommodation Facilites

Does the number of hotels in a particular area have an effect on how many rides occur on road segments located there? We decided to look at the number of hotels as a reference measure to rate how popular an area is for visitors and represent its capability of hosting potential bike riders.

In order to do so, we downloaded a publicly available dataset containing a well-representative list of hotels in Italy along with their corresponding latitude and longitude coordinates. The dataset was downloaded from DatiOpen.it, one of the largest italian initiatives on Open Data.
Initially, we investigated the geographical density of rides correlated with the quantity of hotels in specific areas as shown in the image below.

The heatmap represents the density of rides in the period 2009-2017, with increasing density levels from green to red: the pattern in this case is of course the same as the one displayed in previous image. Orange clusters show instead the number of hotels in a given area, making it possible to devise a first simple (but effective) correlation between accommodations and "ride propensity" on different areas in Italy. As we can see, in general, areas with higher ride densities also feature higher concentrations of hotels, showing that hotels can be adopted as an additional feature to build an effective predictive model.

Then, we leveraged one of KMeans clustering implementations provided by Scikit-learn (BallTree) adopting a distance function relying on geodesic distance (via Geopy library), so as to obtain for each Strava segment a set of statistical values:
  • the nearest 100 hotels;
  • the average distance of those hotels from the segment;
    • < li>the corresponding standard deviation, median, 25 and 75 percentile values;
  • the number hotels within a 30-km radius.
These values were also used later as features for engineering the dataset for the machine-learning model.

Machine Learning

The main goal of the project was to develop a regression model able to predict the number of rides occurring in a specific Strava segment at a given future time. In order to do so, we relied on machine-learning-based approach. The problem faced here was a typical regression problem where the target is represented by number of rides. Additionally, it is worth noting that time plays an important role since we aim at predicting future values: as such it is essential to introduce validation techniques similar to those adopted in time-series analysis.
The most important stage of our work was related to feature engineering since designing good features is a key activity for implementing accurate models.

Feature Engineering

In order to devise an effective set of feature to feed the predictive model at hand, we leveraged the analysis shown previously, resulting in the set of features described below.

Month and Day of Week

These features were included in the set of features since seasonality clearly plays a fundamental role in recreational cyclists' habits: as shown above, indeed, mid seasons and summer are the part of the year where most of the rides tend to occur. Also, weekend is clearly preferred over regural workdays.
Both the features are to be intended as categorical.

Years since Strava Kickoff

We have seen previously that Strava has witnessed an exponential growth in terms of number of rides (km ridden) since its kickoff in 2009: this can be regarded as a true drift we need to be taken into account.
To this end, when trying to predict how many rides will occur on a specific segment in a particular day on a given month, we are also going to consider how many years has passed since Strava openings. In order to do so we can simply take the year and subtract 2009 (the year Strava was launched): accordingly, if we want to predict the number of rides occurring on a segment in the Sundays of August 2019, "years since kickoff" is going to have a value of 10.

Geographical Location of Segments

As seen during analysis, location of Strava segments is a good predictor for assessing the trend of rides on those, as such, we decided to include in the set of features each segment's start latitude and longitude.

Hotels

The availability of accommodation structures is also a factor affecting segments' appeal. Accordingly, we exploited the dataset on Italy's hotels and, for each segment, devised the following set of features:

  • the nearest 100 hotels;
  • the average distance of those hotels from the segment;
  • the corresponding standard deviation, median, 25 and 75 percentile values;
  • the number hotels within a 30-km radius.

Summing up

As a result of feature engineering process, the machine-learning-ready data is composed so as to feature, for each segment, several samples: specifically, one for each day of the week of every month for each of the years from 2009 to 2017 (these are represented in terms of "years passed since Strava launch").
Furthermore, for each sample, reference latitude and longitude, as well as statistical values regarding hotels, are also specified as additional features.

Designing a Regression Model

Well-established machine learning techniques were adopted to devise a predictive model of the number of rides.
The model was developed on Scikit-learn via exploitation of esemble techniques resulting in a combination of two regressors in sequence:

  • a linear regressor built with a Ridge model;
  • a random-forest regressor (RandomForestRegressor) fit on the residuals (errors) resulting from fitting the linear model.

Train/Test Set and Validation

As time plays a key role in predicting number of rides, the full dataset was spit in a train and a test set by relying on an approach typical of time-series analysis: all the samples referring to the period 2009-2016 were included in the train set, while the remaining samples regarding year 2017 went to form the test set: accordingly, the number of samples in the resulting training set was 485,567, while the test set resulted in 201,057 samples.
In fact, given the strong temporal nature of the data, preserving the order of samples is fundamental when splitting the dataset.

As far as validation is concerned, the model was cross-validated adopting a well-established time-series-analysis techniques called forward chaining, which is provided by Scikit-learn in TimeSeriesSplit.
By relying on TimeSeriesSplit, cross-validation was then performed by considering 3 splits and adopting grid search (GridSearchCV on Scikit-learn) to investigate the most-effective set of hyperparameters.

The aforementioned cross-validation process resulted in the following hyperparameter values:

  • A regularization hyperparameter alpha equal to 0.001 on the ridge linear model.
  • As far as random forest is concerned, the following values were instead found:
    • max features = 0.6
    • number of decision trees in the ensemble = 40

Results

Predicting on the test set, the model achieved the following scores:

  • an R2 score or coefficient of determination equal to a 0.67 level of explained variance.
  • A mean absolute error corresponding to 4.3 rides.

Designing the Actual Application

Starting from the regressor model devised, the aim of the project was to build an application to help amateur riders find the most popular places to ride in Italy, according to the period of the year they intend to visit a specific region.
In order to do so, we developed The Cycling Feast Application as an interactive map where the user can specify the month and the year she intends to ride in Italy; then, clicking on a particular italian region, the underlying regressor model will make predictions on a dataset structured in this way: for each Strava segment in the region a sample is created for each day of the week (for a total of 7 samples per segment), adopting the selected month as the value for the month feature and calculating the value of "years since kickoff" accordingly to the year chosen by the user.
The resulting dataset sent to regressor for prediction has a size corresponding to #segments in the region x 7.
The predicted values are then analyzed via a Kernel Density Estimation so as to build a heatmap of ride density in the region. Additionally, the 10 segments featuring the highest number of total rides predicted are shown on the map, so as to help the user decide where to ride. Also, clicking on each "best segment" shows additional details such as, for instance, the predicted distribution of rides over the different days of the week.

Future Work

Where are we going to go from here? This remains indeed a very preliminary work with several further analyses and improvements to be made.
A first improvement would be of course that of expanding the number of segments to account for as well as to further investigate feature engineering. This could involve exploiting new data, for instance data reporting historical weather conditions in Italy.
Then, in order to keep the model always up to date, we will introduce online learning so as to fit the model also on latest rides recorded on Strava.
